Bio

I received a B.S. from Olin College of Engineering, Needham Massachusetts, in 2012—designing my own major in robotics engineering. In the summer of 2012 I joined the Florida Institute for Human and Machine Cognition, and participated in the DARPA virtual robotics challenge—which we won! in the fall of 2013, I joined the Human Centered Robotics Lab at the University of Texas at Austin, eventually obtaining a Ph.D. in Mechanical Engineering in 2019. Between 2015 and 2019 I was funded through a NASA Space Technology Research Fellowship, collaborating with Johnson Space Center's Robotics Team on the control of compliant humanoid robots. Along the way, I discovered that the same mechanical impedance shaping mathematics I had been applying to humanoid robots could also be applied to strength amplifying exoskeletons. In 2018, I started collaborating with Apptronik Inc. (an HCRL spinoff) to apply these techniques to the Sagitarius lower body exoskeleton.

I am currently a postdoctoral research fellow at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, working with Drs. Robert D. Gregg and Elliott Rouse in the Locomotor Control Systems and Neurobionics Laboratory. My research continues to apply my background in estimation and control to wearable robots. Through studying high bandwidth wearable feedback systems, I hope to surpass the perceptual threshold of strength amplification behaviors, and create a class of devices that can function as seamless extensions of the human neuomuscular system.
